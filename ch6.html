This section explains how to resolve and prevent incidents. A customer service analyst must resolve incidents quickly and efficiently. To do this, he follows the incident management process. This process helps to restore service quickly to minimize the impact of the incident. When an incident cannot be resolved immediately, someone must be held accountable. This ensures efficient follow-up. After resolution, all information must be recorded to analyze trends.Problem management helps to identify and prevent future incidents, thereby improving business operations.
Key Words closed loop process—A process that changes its output based on feedback. early life support (ELS)—The stage in the service lifecycle that occurs at the end of a deployment and before the service is fully accepted into operation (ITIL definition). Five Whys—A technique that involves repeatedly asking the question “Why?” until the root cause of a problem is determined IT planner—The individual responsible for producing and maintaining the IT standards, policies, plans, and strategies that ensure the IT department’s services meet the business’ strategic needs Kepner-Tregoe problem analysis—A proprietary problem analysis technique developed by Charles Kepner and Ben Tregoe that involves defining and describing the problem, establishing possible causes, testing the most probable cause, and verifying the true cause. Pareto analysis—A technique for determining the most significant causes from a list of many possible causes of a problem probable source—The system, network, application, or product that is most likely causing an incident. request for change (RFC)—A request to change the production environment. standard change—A preapproved change that is low risk and follows a procedure. status data—The details about an incident that are used to track the incident throughout its lifecycle. target escalation time—A time constraint placed on each level that ensures incident resolution activities are proceeding at an appropriate pace.
